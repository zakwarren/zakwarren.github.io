Layout and Design

Documentation 30 Aug 2021

As well as the content itself, we need to think about the layout and design of the document as a whole. Information can be communicated more easily with a good design than it can with a bad one.

Typography

Typography is what language looks like. The choice of font, its size, and spacing can do a lot to keep a user engaged or to push them away. There are two main types of font, serif and sans serif, and two lesser used types, script and decorative.

Serif

Serif fonts are those with the extra shapes and flourishes on individual letters. The key points to keep in mind with serif fonts are:

  • best used in printed materials, as the high resolution of print makes it very readable
  • good for headings
  • ensure that they have a font size of 12 or more
  • risks looking amateurish on the web
  • not liked by all users

Sans Serif

Sans serif fonts are those without the extra shapes or flourishes on individual letters. The key points to keep in mind with sans serif fonts are:

  • best used on the web, as they are readable even on low resolution screens
  • can be used with most font sizes, including very small
  • they have a professional, clean look
  • widely liked by users

Script and Decorative

These are fonts that can look like handwriting, have very fancy styling, or are otherwise decorative. The key points to keep in mind with script and decorative fonts are:

  • use in very limited amounts, if at all
  • not recommended for serious documentation
  • can hinder legibility
  • can enhance a design in specific circumstances

Fundamental Design Principles

There are four main design principles that we can use to make our documentation better.

Alignment

Alignment can create strength and cohesion in a design. Text can be aligned:

  • left
  • right
  • centred

Proximity

Proximity implies a relationship or lack thereof. Parts that are closer to each other imply that they are more closely related than those that are far apart.

Contrast

Contrast can be in:

  • colour
  • shape
  • size

It helps information pop and grab the reader’s attention. It’s also good for creating a visual hierarchy.

Whitespace

Whitespace is simple, but powerful. It’s a way to draw attention to certain elements and to break up sections so they don’t overwhelm a reader.
