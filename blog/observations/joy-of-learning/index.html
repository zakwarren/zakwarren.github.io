The Joy of Learning

Observations 16 October 2020

One of the great joys of software development is that there is always so much to learn. You have to be a life-long learner to be a good software developer. Technologies move on. Some disappear altogether. New things arrive that users and businesses want.

The vast majority of software developers I know are avid learners. I’m the same. Learning new things is fun, a compulsion even. We get a rush when we finally figure something out and all our studies come together for us.

For some, it can be daunting. They want a stable job where they know what they’re getting each day, work at it for a few decades, then retire. New things and change can be scary.

At the end of last year I worked on a spin off app to one of the company’s oldest products. The lead developer on it had been working on this product with the same couple of developers for about thirty years. I found the code hard to read, the architecture arcane and archaic, and the idea of working on the same thing in the same way for so long so strange and hard to comprehend. This project was decreasingly relevant to the business due to this resistance to adapt, it, and the associated way of working was comforting for them.

Learning is also a skill, and one we’re not taught to do particularly well. I spend a lot of time these days training and mentoring placement students and junior developers. I spend a lot of time when new juniors join guiding them to find out how they best learn and teaching them skills to help them learn better. One of my catchphrases seems to have become “Your main job as a junior developer is to learn as much as you can”.

The act of developing software is primarily the task of learning what it is you’re going to create and what is most useful for the users. If the project is anything more than the smallest, most simple piece of software, then no one knows exactly what it is they want made when you start. This is why working in a way that is optimised for learning is so important.

So how do we do that? We need feedback. We need it regularly and as early as possible.
